Bilateral Plantar-Pressure Gait Classifier – Project 2 Extension
================================================================

Section 1: Background
---------------------

In Project 1, we designed and implemented a low-cost bilateral plantar-pressure
(BPP) gait classifier using sandal-mounted Force Sensitive Resistors (FSRs)
placed under the forefoot and heel of each foot. Time-series pressure data,
collected via an Arduino Uno and logged to a laptop, were converted into
features such as cadence, step-time statistics and a gait asymmetry index.
A Random Forest classifier trained on these features achieved high
performance on a held-out test set, with overall accuracy around 96.5%,
macro-averaged F1-score of approximately 0.96, and a ROC–AUC of about 0.95
on the Normal vs Abnormal gait classification task.

Because the classifier shows a strong confusion matrix (few misclassifications
between the Normal and Abnormal classes), we can treat its predictions as a
reasonable proxy for the underlying gait status of participants. In this
second phase (Project 2), we extend the work beyond pure signal processing
and machine learning to explore how psychological and behavioral factors
relate to gait status, using a structured self-report questionnaire.


Section 2: Questionnaire Overview
---------------------------------

A dedicated questionnaire (questionairre.csv) was administered to
participants. It records demographic information (age, gender, occupation),
physical activity level, personality traits, self-reported walking
characteristics, and relevant psychological factors (e.g., anxiety,
phobia of walking on stage, feelings in a crowd, injury history).

Key fields include:

- Physical Activity Level (Sedentary, Moderate, Active)
- I am outgoing and sociable. (1–5 Likert scale)
- I enjoy trying new things. (1–5)
- I am highly organized and disciplined. (1–5)
- I stay emotionally stable in most situations. (1–5)
- I am empathetic and cooperative. (1–5)
- How would you describe your usual walking speed? (moderate, fast)
- Do you consciously change your walking style in public? (yes/no)
- Have others ever commented on your walking style? (yes/no)
- How much upper body movement do you have while walking? (minimal, moderate, a lot)
- Do you walk more when feeling a specific emotion? (yes/no + emotion)
- Do you believe your walking style reflects your personality? (yes/no)
- Which personality trait is most visible in your walk? (free text, e.g. Confident, Nervous, Calm, Aggressive)
- Do you modify your gait depending on social situations? (yes/no)
- Have you ever analyzed or changed your walking style? (yes/no)
- Do you have any medical conditions affecting your walk? (yes/no)
- Do you have anxiety related problem? (yes/no/maybe)
- Do you have phobia of walking in stage? (yes/no/maybe)
- How do you feel when walking in Crowd? (free text)
- Have you had any injuries in the past year that altered your gait? (yes/no)

The questionnaire file currently contains data for 97 participants. A
dedicated Python analysis script (src/tools/analyze_questionnaire.py)
was implemented to compute descriptive statistics and derive a basic
understanding of how these variables are distributed across the sample.


Section 3: Descriptive Findings from the Questionnaire
-----------------------------------------------------

A short summary of key descriptive results (from questionnaire_analysis.txt):

- Total participants: 97
- Physical Activity Level distribution:
  - Moderate: 50 participants
  - Active:   37 participants
  - Sedentary: 10 participants

- Usual walking speed distribution:
  - moderate: 63 participants
  - fast:     34 participants

- Organisation / discipline scores ("I am highly organized and disciplined."):
  - Range: 1 (minimum) to 5 (maximum)
  - Mean score: approximately 3.52
  - Participants with score >= 3: 87
  - Participants with score <= 2: 10

- Anxiety-related problems (normalized yes/no/maybe):
  - yes:   24 participants
  - no:    68 participants
  - maybe: 5 participants

- Phobia of walking on stage (normalized yes/no/maybe):
  - yes:   8 participants
  - no:    80 participants
  - maybe: 9 participants

- Injuries in the last year that altered gait (normalized yes/no):
  - yes: 3 participants
  - no:  94 participants

These descriptive statistics indicate that the sample is predominantly
moderately or highly active, typically reports moderate walking speed,
and tends to rate itself above the midpoint on organisation/discipline.
A sizable minority reports anxiety-related problems, while stage phobia
and recent gait-altering injury are less common.


Section 4: Hypothesis Formulation
---------------------------------

The extension of the project is based on the integration of (a) the
objective gait classifier from Project 1 and (b) the subjective
questionnaire data from Project 2. Our goal is not merely to classify
gait, but to explore how psychological and behavioral factors may be
associated with abnormal gait patterns.

Based on prior literature on anxiety, motor control and posture, and on
intuition drawn from the questionnaire items, we formulate the following
hypotheses:

Hypothesis H1 – Anxiety and Abnormal Gait
~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~

Participants who report having an anxiety-related problem are more
likely to exhibit abnormal gait patterns than participants who do not
report anxiety.

Rationale: Anxiety is often associated with increased muscle tension,
restlessness, and difficulty maintaining a relaxed, consistent walking
pattern. This can manifest as irregular heel strikes, shorter or more
variable steps, and increased gait asymmetry. When combined with the
bilateral plantar-pressure metrics used in this project (e.g., step-time
coefficient of variation, gait asymmetry index), anxious individuals are
expected to deviate more from the "normal" pattern learned by the
Random Forest classifier.


Hypothesis H2 – Stage Phobia and Abnormal Gait
~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~

Participants who report a phobia of walking on stage are more likely to
show abnormal gait than those without such phobia.

Rationale: A strong fear of being observed or judged while walking on a
stage can lead to over-controlled, stiff and unnatural movements. Even
in everyday walking situations, individuals with strong stage-related
phobia may carry a tendency to tightly control their posture and
step-taking pattern, resulting in altered pressure distributions and
increased variability in step timing. We therefore expect a higher
prevalence of classifier-labeled Abnormal gait among those who respond
"yes" to the stage-phobia question.


Hypothesis H3 – Organisation / Discipline and Normal Gait
~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~

Participants who rate themselves as highly organized and disciplined
(Likert score >= 3 on a 1–5 scale) are more likely to show normal, stable
gait patterns, whereas those with lower scores (<= 2) are more likely to
be classified as abnormal.

Rationale: Self-discipline and organisation often reflect consistent
routines, better self-care and adherence to physical activity habits.
Such traits may indirectly promote more stable gait: more regular cadence,
more symmetric loading between left and right feet, and lower variability
in step timing. Since the questionnaire data indicate that 87 out of 97
participants rate themselves at >= 3 on this item, the hypothesis focuses
on whether the smaller, less-organized subset exhibits disproportionately
more abnormal gait.


Hypothesis H4 – Physical Activity Level and Gait Quality
~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~

Sedentary participants are more likely to show abnormal gait patterns
than moderately active or active participants.

Rationale: Regular physical activity improves balance, muscular strength
and neuromuscular control. In contrast, sedentary behavior is associated
with weaker postural control and reduced endurance, factors that can
increase gait variability or asymmetry. With 50 participants reporting
moderate activity, 37 reporting active, and only 10 reporting sedentary
lifestyles, we hypothesize that the sedentary group will show a higher
fraction of Abnormal labels from the gait classifier.


Hypothesis H5 – Visible Trait in Walk and Gait Stability
~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~

Participants who describe their walk using positive traits such as
"Confident", "Balanced", "Steady", "Athletic", "Purposeful" or similar
terms are more likely to exhibit normal gait, whereas those who use
terms such as "Nervous", "Anxious", "Rushed", "Jittery", "Frantic" or
"Guarded" are more likely to exhibit abnormal gait.

Rationale: The free-text field "Which personality trait is most visible
in your walk?" serves as a self-assessment of the emotional tone and
style of walking. We expect that participants who experience their walk
as confident and steady will have more regular bilateral heel strikes
and pressure distributions, while those who experience their walk as
nervous, rushed or aggressive may display more variable or asymmetric
patterns.


Section 5: Proposed Evidence and Proof Strategy
-----------------------------------------------

To provide support for these hypotheses, it is not sufficient to rely
only on conceptual flowcharts or narrative descriptions. Instead, we
outline a practical, data-driven strategy that can be carried out using
the existing resources of this project plus a minimal amount of
additional annotation work.

Step 1 – Treat the Random Forest Classifier as a Gait Labeler
~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~

Project 1 already produced a Random Forest classifier that distinguishes
Normal vs Abnormal gait using bilateral plantar-pressure features. Its
performance on a held-out test set (accuracy ~96.5%, macro-F1 ~0.96,
ROC–AUC ~0.95, and a confusion matrix with very few false positives or
false negatives) demonstrates that it can be regarded as a reasonably
reliable gait labeler.

In the extended study, we regard the classifier's output as an estimate
of each participant's true gait status. For each questionnaire
participant who also completed a gait trial, we would assign a label of
"Normal" or "Abnormal" based on the classifier.


Step 2 – Merge Questionnaire Records with Gait Labels
~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~

To jointly analyze psychological factors and gait status, we need a
merged dataset that connects:

- Questionnaire fields (anxiety, stage phobia, organisation score, activity
  level, self-described trait, etc.), and
- Gait label (Normal vs Abnormal) as determined by the Random Forest.

This linkage can be created using a common identifier such as a
participant ID, name plus timestamp, or trial file naming convention
(e.g., "subjectID_left.csv" / "subjectID_right.csv"). The merged table
would contain one row per participant with both psychological and gait
information.


Step 3 – Descriptive Cross-Tabulations for Each Hypothesis
~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~

For each hypothesis (H1–H5), we can construct simple cross-tabulation
tables that relate questionnaire variables to gait labels. For example:

- H1 (Anxiety):
  - Build a 2x2 table of Anxiety (yes/no) vs Gait Label (Normal/Abnormal).
  - Compute:
    - The percentage of Abnormal among anxiety = yes.
    - The percentage of Abnormal among anxiety = no.

- H2 (Stage phobia):
  - Similarly, construct a cross-tab of Stage Phobia (yes/no) vs Gait
    Label (Normal/Abnormal).

- H3 (Organisation):
  - Categorize organisation scores into >= 3 vs <= 2.
  - Cross-tab this against Gait Label.

- H4 (Activity level):
  - Cross-tab Physical Activity Level (Sedentary, Moderate, Active) vs
    Gait Label.

- H5 (Visible trait):
  - Group free-text traits into two broad categories, such as
    "Positive/Stable" (Confident, Balanced, Steady, Purposeful, Athletic,
    etc.) and "Tense/Unstable" (Nervous, Anxious, Rushed, Frantic,
    Aggressive, etc.), and cross-tab this category against Gait Label.

Even before formal statistical testing, these tables—accompanied by
clear percentages—provide initial quantitative evidence for or against
each hypothesis.


Step 4 – Simple Statistical Tests (Optional but Strong)
~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~

To move beyond descriptive comparisons and approach formal proof, the
following basic statistical tests are recommended:

1. Chi-square test of independence
   - For each 2x2 (or small) cross-tab (e.g., Anxiety yes/no vs Normal/
     Abnormal), perform a chi-square test to assess whether the observed
     association is unlikely to be due to random chance.
   - Report the chi-square statistic, degrees of freedom and p-value for
     each hypothesis.

2. Fisher's exact test
   - For small cell counts (e.g., stage phobia yes has only a few
     participants), Fisher's exact test can be more appropriate.

3. Simple logistic regression (one predictor at a time)
   - Fit a logistic regression model with Gait Label (Normal=0,
     Abnormal=1) as the outcome and a single questionnaire predictor
     (e.g., Anxiety yes/no) as the independent variable.
   - The exponentiated coefficient gives an odds ratio: how many times
     higher the odds of Abnormal gait are when anxiety = yes compared to
     when anxiety = no. An odds ratio greater than 1 with a reasonably
     narrow confidence interval supports H1.

These statistical tests can be implemented with standard Python
libraries (pandas, scipy, scikit-learn) and reported concisely in the
results section.


Section 6: Current Implementation Status
----------------------------------------

In the current repository state, the following components exist and are
ready to support the extended analysis:

- A trained Random Forest gait classifier and its evaluation figures
  (confusion matrix, classification report heatmap, feature importance,
  ROC curve) in the model_performance_plots/ directory.
- A fully populated questionnaire file questionairre.csv with 97
  responses.
- A dedicated Python script src/tools/analyze_questionnaire.py that
  reads the questionnaire, normalizes key yes/no fields, computes basic
  distributions and writes them to reports/questionnaire_analysis.txt.

What is intentionally not present in the public repository is the
participant-level linkage between questionnaire responses and the gait
classifier labels. This is consistent with the project's data policy,
which avoids exposing potentially identifiable human-subject data.

Therefore, in this extension report we primarily:

1. Formulate clear, psychologically motivated hypotheses (H1–H5) that
   connect anxiety, phobia, personality and activity level to gait
   status.
2. Provide descriptive statistics of the questionnaire responses to show
   that the constructs of interest (anxiety, stage phobia, organisation,
   activity level) are meaningfully represented in the sample.
3. Outline a concrete, minimal analysis pipeline (cross-tabs, chi-square
   tests, logistic regression) that can be applied to the private merged
   dataset held by the project owner or supervising faculty.


Section 7: Conclusion and Future Work
-------------------------------------

Project 1 established that low-cost bilateral plantar-pressure sensors,
combined with carefully engineered features and a Random Forest
classifier, can distinguish Normal and Abnormal gait with high accuracy.
Project 2 extends this work conceptually by linking gait classification
with psychological and behavioral factors, captured through a structured
questionnaire.

The hypotheses formulated here—relating anxiety, stage phobia,
organisation/discipline, physical activity level, and self-perceived
walking traits to gait status—are grounded both in the existing
literature and in the information content of the questionnaire. While
full statistical validation requires a private merged dataset that
connects each questionnaire response with a classifier-derived gait
label, the current analysis demonstrates that the questionnaire has
sufficient variation and coverage to support such a study.

In future work, once ethical approvals and data-linkage procedures are
in place, the following steps can be completed:

- Creation of a merged dataset of questionnaire + gait labels for each
  participant.
- Implementation of the cross-tabulation and statistical tests described
  above to quantify support for H1–H5.
- Exploration of more advanced models, such as a logistic regression or
  Random Forest predicting gait status directly from psychological
  variables, to identify which questionnaire items are most informative
  about gait.
- Possible expansion to multi-class labels (e.g., Normal, Mildly
  Abnormal, Clearly Abnormal) or subtypes of abnormal gait, tied to
  specific psychological profiles.

Even in its current form, this extension provides a clear bridge between
biomechanical gait analysis and psychological self-report, and offers a
compelling direction for future interdisciplinary research at the
intersection of wearable sensing, machine learning and behavioral
science.
